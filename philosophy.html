Teaching Philosophy

I believe that student engagement is the foremost important element of teaching and learning and the simplest way to ensure students are gaining something from the class.

I endeavor to maximize engagement through demonstrated enthusiasm for the material, regular, routinized dialogue with students, and a supportive environment where mistakes are treated as learning opportunities. I have always felt that if I am bored teaching a class, I can be assured the students are also bored, which means they are not learning. When I find the material I am teaching to be interesting, exciting, and relevant, my intellectual energy keeps students awake and focused; I believe fun and learning are not mutually exclusive, and I expect my classes to be characterized by "joyful rigor."

In order to maintain student engagement, my most relied upon tool is frequent, non-volitional questioning. By setting a classroom environment where anybody can be expected to be called upon at any time, students quickly learn to be active listeners and ask questions when they are unclear. I have found that a combination of open-ended questions and targeted inquiries directed toward specific individuals works best to challenge students at their individual levels of understanding.

However, for students to be truly engaged, they must also feel they have the proper tools and knowledge to discuss and analyze concepts. This is why I feel that at every level of education, scaffolding is essential, especially when introducing a subject such as economics, which includes concepts that are not always intuitive. For my classes, this includes sufficient modeling of quantitative economic models, guided step-by-step tasks in creating diagrams, and consistent practice through formative assignments.

When students are given enough support when introduced to new ideas initially, it results in solid conceptual understanding and the ability to apply logic using sound reasoning and analysis.

Additionally, students must be made to feel safe to make mistakes along this way. I achieve this through teacher demonstration of best practices when making an error, openly acknowledging that everyone makes mistakes and modeling how to learn from them. I seek to set a growth-mindset culture from day one, which assumes that mistakes are an inevitable and valuable part of the learning process. I tell my students that if they aren’t making mistakes, then the material is likely too easy for them.

Finally, no student will maintain interest in a subject unless they can find personal relevance. Finding connections between the material and current economic news serves the dual purpose of grounding abstract theory in reality and encouraging students to analyze global and local developments through the lens of their academic endeavors.

My focus is on developing student skills beyond mere memorization, moving toward a level where they are able to apply ideas and use them in meaningful evaluation. I believe that student engagement is the key, and these are a few of the ways I ensure my students are active participants in their own educational journey.
